Solving PDEs with incomplete information¶

  • Peter Binev, Andrea Bonito, Albert Cohen, Wolfgang Dahmen, Ronald Devore and Guergana Petrove

Formulation of problem¶

  • Elliptic problem with incomplete boundary conditions

\begin{equation*} - \Delta u = f,\quad x\in {\Omega}, \quad u = g,\quad, x\in \Gamma = \partial{\Omega} \end{equation*} The source term \(f\) is known and \(g\) is unknown and is some function in \(K^B\), ball of a space that compactly imported in \(H^{\frac12}(\Gamma)\) One typical choice is the unit ball \(U(H^s(\Gamma))\) in \(H^s(\Gamma)\).

Harmonic recovery¶

Introducing the space of harmonic functions \(\mathcal{H}^s({\Omega})\), which is also the extension of \(H^s(\Gamma)\) by harmonic extension. The norm of \(\mathcal{H}^s({\Omega} )\) is defined by the boundary norm. According to the smoothness of the domain boundary, we have the interpolation \begin{equation*} \mathcal{H}^s({\Omega})\subset H^r({\Omega}) \end{equation*} with \(r=\min\{s+\frac12,r^*\}\)

  • For smooth domain, \(r^*=\infty\)

  • For polygons, \(r^* = 1+ \frac{\pi}{\omega}-\varepsilon\) where \(\omega\) is the largest inner angle.

Example: \(\omega = \frac{\pi}{2}\), which is convex, then \(r^*\approx 2\)

The task is to find the Chebyshev center in \({H}^1\) which satisfies the observations (in a linear space) \begin{equation*} \lambda_j( \phi) = w_j \end{equation*} The candidate set is denoted as \(\mathcal{K}_w = \{u\in \mathcal{H}^s: \lambda(u) = w\}\).

First in the hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}^s\), the Chebyshev center coincides with the minimal norm interpolant \begin{equation*} u^*(w) = \textrm{argmin} \{ \| v\|_{\mathcal{H}^s}: v\in \mathcal{H}^s, \ \lambda(v) = w\} \end{equation*}

Due to the symmetry of \(\mathcal{K}_w\), its Chebyshev center in \(H^1\) is its geometrical center and the Chebyshev center in \(\mathcal{H}^s\)

Finally, to solve the minimal norm interpolant, we apply the \(L^2\) projection in the Hilbert space, where the finite dimensional space is spanned by the Riesz representers of functionals \(\lambda\)

Computation of Riesz representer in Harmonic space¶

  • Exact riesz representer

\begin{align*} & \phi = E \psi,\\ & \langle \psi, \eta \rangle_{H^s(\Gamma)} = \lambda(E\eta),\quad \forall \eta\in H^s(\Gamma) \end{align*} which means solving boundary representer first and exnteded to the domain

  • Finite element setting

Consider a finite element space \(V_h\) associated with a family of meshes \(\mathcal{T}_h\) of \({\Omega}\). The boundary space \(T_h = T(V_h)\). Domain space with Dirichlet boundary condition \(W_h = \{v_h\in V_h: T(v_h) = 0\}\).

  • The constrain of \(P_k\) Lagrange element on the boundary belongs to \(H^{\frac32-}(\Gamma)\). Then \(T_h\subset H^s(\Gamma)\) for \(s<\frac32\).

  • Numerical Harmonic extension \(E_h: T_h\to V_h\)

\begin{equation*} \int_ {\Omega} \nabla E_h g_h\cdot \nabla v_h = 0,\quad v_h \in W_h \end{equation*} * Riesz representer

\begin{align*} & \phi_h = E_h \psi_h,\\ & \langle \psi_h,g_h \rangle_{H^s(\Gamma)} = \lambda(E_h g_h),\quad g_h\in T_h \end{align*}

  • Saddle point formulation

Introduce \(X^s= \{v\in H^1( {\Omega}): v_ \Gamma \in H^s( \Gamma)\}\) (bigger than \(H^1({\Omega})\cap \mathcal{H}^s({\Omega}))\), equipped with the norm \begin{equation*} \| v\|_{X^s({\Omega})} = \left( \| v_ \Gamma\|_{H^s(\Gamma)}^2 + \| \nabla v\|_{L^2({\Omega})}^2\right)^{\frac12} \end{equation*} Note that \(\| v\|_{\mathcal{H}^s} = \| v\|_{H^s(\Gamma)}\) controls the \(L^2\) norm

The saddle point problem formulates as find \((\phi,\pi)\in X^s({\Omega})\times H^1({\Omega})\) such that \begin{align*} & a(\phi,v) + b(v,\pi) = \lambda(v),\quad v\in X^s({\Omega})\\ & b(\phi,z) = 0, z\in H_0^1({\Omega}) \end{align*} where \begin{align*} a(\phi,v) = \langle \phi_ \Gamma, v_ \Gamma \rangle_{H^s(\Gamma)},\quad b(v, \pi) = \langle \nabla v, \nabla \pi \rangle_{L^2({\Omega})} \end{align*} Testing \(v\in \mathcal{H}^s({\Omega})\) in the first equation leads to \(a( \phi,v) = \lambda(v)\) * Continuity of \(a\) and \(b\); Coercivity of \(a\) on the null space of \(b\) in \(X^s({\Omega})\), null space is \(\mathcal{H}^s({\Omega})\)

For \(v\in \mathcal{H}^s({\Omega})\), one has \begin{equation*} \| v\|_{X^s({\Omega})}^s \le \| v_ \Gamma\|_{H^s(\Gamma)}^2 + \| v\|_{H^1}^2 \le (1+C^2) \| v_ \Gamma\|_{H^s(\Gamma)}^2\le (1+C^2)a(v,v) \end{equation*} by using the embedding \(H^s(\Gamma)\to H^\frac12(\Gamma)\)

Stability and convergence¶

  • Stability of \(E\) Since \(E\) is to solve the Laplace equation, we obtain the stability \begin{equation*} \| Eg\|_{H^1({\Omega})} \lesssim \| g\|_{H^\frac12(\Gamma)} \end{equation*}

  • Stability of \(E_h\) If there exists uniformly \(H^1\) stable projections \(P_h: H^1({\Omega})\to V_h\) that preserves the homogeneous boundary condition (to apply Poincare inequality and \(\|\nabla E_h g_h\|\le \|\nabla P_h Eg_h\|\)) \begin{equation*} P_h(H_0^1({\Omega})) = W_h,\quad \| P_hv\|_{H^1}\le B \| v\|_{H^1} \end{equation*} where \(B\) is independent of \(h\). This guarantees the stability of \(E_h:T_h\to V_h\) \begin{align*} \| E_h g_h - P_hEg_h\|_{H^1}\le 2C_P \| P_h Eg_h\|_{H^1} \end{align*}

  • Error in replacing \(E\) by \(E_h\). For any \(g_h\in T_h\), one has \begin{align*} \| (E-E_h) g_h\|_{H^1({\Omega})} \le C(r,s, \sigma, {\Omega}) h^{r-1} \| g_h\|_{H^s(\Gamma)} \end{align*}

  • Regularity of boundary Riesz representer \begin{equation*} \langle \kappa, v \rangle_{H^s(\Gamma)} = \lambda(v),\quad v\in H^s(\Gamma) \end{equation*}

    If \(\lambda\in H^{-s}(\Gamma)\), then \(\kappa \in H^s(\Gamma)\).

    If \(\lambda\in L^2(\Gamma)\), then \(\kappa\in H^1(\Gamma)\) and \(\kappa \in H^2(E)\) for each edge \(E\) such that the standard finite element approximation estimes yield \begin{equation*} \min_{\kappa_h\in T_h} \| \kappa - \kappa_h\|_{H^1(\Gamma)}\le Ch \| g\|_{L^2(\Gamma)} \end{equation*}

  • More on regularity

    Consider the two point boundary value problem with discontinuous \(\kappa\) such that

\begin{equation*} \kappa = 1_{[0,0.5)} + 2*1_{[0.5,1]} \end{equation*} The weak form writes

\begin{equation*} \int_0^1 \kappa u^\prime v^\prime = \int_0^1 f v \end{equation*} for \(v\in H_0^1\)

A typical choice is choosing \(f = -\frac14x^{-\frac54}\), as the derivative of \(g = x^{-\frac14}\). By Fourier transform, \(x^{-\alpha}\to \xi^{-(n-\alpha)}\), then \({\mathcal{F}} g = \xi^{-\frac34}\) and allows to take \(\frac14\) derivatives Means \(g\in H^{\frac14-}\). Then \(f\in H^{-\frac34-}\). Then \(u\in H^{\frac14-}\) (no elliptic regularity improvement, only valid in two sub-intervals) Then linear approximation error is \(h^{\frac14}\).
